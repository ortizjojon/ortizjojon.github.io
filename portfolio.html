  • Crimson Moon

    • Description

    A simple Turn Based RPG Game(Boss Fight), where the user plays as a Knight fighting against a dark cult leader,"The Goat",to stop the crimson moon from destroying the world. For every attack you make on the Boss, you are scored points. All of the decisions made by the enemy are random, having 4 options.(Attack,Raise Attack,Lower Attack,Heal) Randomness can also occur from how much damage you deal and take. When the boss's health reaches at a certain point, he summons an entity called "The Book" granting him higher attack power and a higher chance of attacking the user.This ups the difficulty and is where most people lose, myself included. The user loses when their health reaches 0, showing a game over screen which plays an 8-Bit version of Soundgarden's "Black Hole Sun".

    Reflection

    I created the game out of love for some Turn Based RPGs.The direction I was heading the game towards was more of a dark theme with minor references to roguelike medias. Two main successful points in the developement process were getting the general playstyle in first before I got into making the sprites and designs. Another would also be giving the game it's retro feel, using third party programs to create sprites for the game and throwing in 8-Bit versions of Rock songs polished the game. I did however come across some issues with the program itself, such as having some animations not correspond with thier proper role, recieving extra turns after certain actions and even having the game being utterly IMPOSSIBLE to beat. These challenges took a while to overcome, but with more time, variables, math corrections and runthroughs, I now have a complete game. With the game being finished and all, if I the time and resources I think I would add a full party for the user to use, a team of four instead of just one hero. It would've made for more variety and unique decision making for the player. I'd also consider for myself to add more of a rewarding experience for beating the game, apart from a Congrats.

    More Explanations

    The Algorithm above contributes significantly above to the game's main play style. It dictates the enemy's decision making and broadcasts different choices. After the enemy's turn is finished, it switchs to the players turn and the enemy waits until an option is completed.

    Every block corresponds to the actions made by the enemy. Variables are then changed from that action.




    Three of Swords

    Description

    Two wanderers stumble across a village in search of rest for the night. The further they dwell into this mysterious town, the more secrets they uncover and learn of the town's sacred curse by the tarot card , "Three of Swords".

    (Click on the sample flow chart to view.)

    Discussion

    Throughout the process of making the game, me and my partner encountered many challenges involving the story and programming. An incremental developement process we faced was writing the story. When we first started the project, we had no idea what we wanted to write and had no clear direction where the story should go. Over time however, we continously added on more ideas in to the process where we finally reached a real theme, which became the main focus of our story that had centered around this one idea(The meanings of the Tarot Card). An iterative developement process we also faced was continously writing "line''" commands that produce the words of the story character by character and defining the many paths that were written into our program. Patience was definitely important to finishing our project which would've resulted in a rushed program had we not taken the time into writing our story and making sure the codes were working. Addtionally,we had to keep looking back to our flow-chart which kept track of all our events and paths. Organizing scenes and functions had greatly benefitted us during our developement and was a procedural abstraction from the layout of our flow-chart, allowing us more of an oppurtunity to come back and add more onto other events in the story without losing track of what we're doing.
